Final Reflection

Coming up with the plots was actually more difficult than I initially imagined. I had many ideas that honestly didn’t show me data in the way in which I expected them to. For example, initially I explored different ways that we can see racial bias in the NYPD. I categorized a set of outcomes as severe and another set as mild. I then checked to see if certain race groups' complaints had disparities in their outcomes, but this resulted in a very similar distribution in mild and severe outcomes between all races. Because of this I have to look for other avenues that could substantiate my proposition. I was able to do that by looking at distributions per race and see how they changed in different metrics over time, which allowed me to arrive at my final conclusions. I was honestly really surprised by the fact that the race of the person making the complaint didn’t affect the outcome on police officers, if the claim was substantiated.
I think ethical analysis is difficult to define. What some may see as deceptive, others may not. But I think it's essentially, scrutinizing visualization heavily before you take their implications as a fact. I think persuasive choices should be okay if they reflect that data we see at large. Within my data set I would say that the FOR proposition is less deceptive since the Substantiation rate graph reflects the larger trend but zooms in to be more persuasive. I think selecting data that is an outlier and showing it as fact is misleading in many ways. This allows viewers to generalize that outlier to the larger trend without necessarily knowing that it was an outlier to make a point. Essentially, showing all your data is important and that could prevent misleading but we can never really know if all the data was included or not. But overall, I think if a visualization is suggesting something that isn’t reflected in the larger trend of data then you can say that it's misleading.
